## Post 1

**Date:** 2026-04-01
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_ive-seen-clients-with-a-08-positive-reply-activity-7445092760828723200-L69g?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADdny6sBh3WUw6nkJ1q2svhpEWlYntNC2UE 

---

I've seen clients with a 0.8% positive reply rate printing money.

I've seen clients with a 4% positive reply rate struggling to stay afloat.

The difference? One was selling $80K contracts. The other was selling a $500 product.

Reply rate is a vanity metric if you don't connect it to revenue.

Here's what to calculate instead:

1. Size your TAM. How many companies actually fit your ICP? If it's 3,000, you'll burn through them in 2 months at scale. Plan accordingly.

2. Find your positive reply rate. Not total reply rate — positive replies divided by emails sent. That's your real conversion.

3. Multiply by deal value and close rate. One positive reply on an $80K deal with a 25% close rate is worth $20K. One positive reply on a $500 deal with a 20% close rate is worth $100.

Those are fundamentally different businesses that require fundamentally different outbound strategies.

The $80K business needs precision targeting and can afford to send fewer, better emails. The $500 business needs massive volume and tight unit economics to make cold email work at all.

Before your next campaign: how much is one positive reply actually worth to your business?

That number changes everything about how you run outbound.

---

**Why relevant:** Reframes reply rate as a vanity metric and introduces a unit economics framework (TAM size × positive reply rate × deal value × close rate) for evaluating cold email viability. 
Essential thinking for any B2B SaaS team deciding whether to invest in outbound and how to size their campaign strategy accordingly.

Here's a strategy to turn your lead list into five or more experiments with Claude Code and Clay.

One thing we think about a lot with our customers is how the list is the message. I don't know who said that first, but it definitely wasn't me, so credit to whoever coined it.

What we mean is this: if you know you can sell to any founder with less than 20 employees, that's a pretty big list. 

But if you know the best founder for you is someone who has founded a company before, has been in business less than two years, has 10 to 20 employees, and has no accounting or finance job titles on their LinkedIn company page...

That person should be receiving a completely different message than someone who has one or two of those traits but not all of them.

We use Claude Code or Clay to enrich our lists for any criteria that might change how we'd message somebody.

For example, if somebody has been in business for two years, they're just getting systems ramped up. That's a way different message than somebody who's been in business for 10 years and has figured all this out already. 

If somebody is a CEO versus a founder, and there's no reason to change your message, then there's no reason to separate them in the list.

We separate the list by what data points change our messaging.

This way we can slice and dice our data and have different email campaigns for multiple layers of ICP fit. Sometimes your golden ICP (where everything is true about that person) might only have a thousand leads in the entire campaign. 

But I guarantee you'll get a higher response rate on those leads than on your broader ones, because you can niche your messaging down more and more.

The list is the message. Use that when creating your campaigns.

---

**Why relevant:** Introduces the "list is the message" framework — using Clay and Claude Code to segment lead lists by data points that change messaging, creating multiple targeted campaigns by ICP fit tier. 
Directly actionable for B2B SaaS teams building outbound systems that go beyond one-size-fits-all sequences.

## Post 3

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_ive-changed-my-mind-on-a-lot-of-things-when-activity-7443284459396857856-_siN?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADdny6sBh3WUw6nkJ1q2svhpEWlYntNC2UE

---

I've changed my mind on a lot of things when it comes to cold email best practices. But these 3 principles have never changed. Not once.

Everything else people sell courses about? Noise on top of these.

1. Deliverability

If your emails don't land in the primary tab, nothing else matters. Not your copy. Not your list. Not your offer.

We always keep the amount of inboxes we need to send for a customer plus anywhere from 50 to 100% extra capacity warming up. That way we can switch out anything that's not working immediately. We monitor this daily for customers having issues and weekly across all customers automatically with Claude skills.

Most people skip this because it's boring. That's why most people's campaigns don't work.

2. The list

Accurate data on the right people. What doesn't work is a mediocre list with 30% bad contacts in it. You should spend as much time on list building as copywriting.

3. Copy with a clear offer that is easy to say yes to

There's a huge difference between copy that sounds clever and copy that converts cold traffic.

I think Alex Hormozi's framework — "We help customers accomplish their goal in this time without this risk" — is the perfect offer framework for cold email. But you also need to think about what's easy to say yes to. If you pitch "We help B2B companies speed up their sales process with a new CRM in 30 days without losing sales momentum," that's still a very tough ask. Changing out somebody's entire CRM is a big commitment. You want to go in with something easier to say yes to.

Shout out David Ogilvy — "your first line gets them to read the second line, and the second line gets them to read the third line" — one of my guiding frameworks. And Josh Braun is one of the best in the game at cold email copywriting if you want to study someone.

People ask me about send times, follow-up cadences, A/B test frameworks, personalization tokens. Those are all optimizations on top of these 3 things.

If your deliverability is broken, your list is wrong, or your copy doesn't resonate. no optimization saves you.

Get these 3 right and you're about 80% of the way to what most agencies charge $10K+/month to do.

---

**Why relevant:** Distills cold outreach down to the three non-negotiable pillars — deliverability, list quality, and copy with a clear offer — and explicitly frames everything else as optimization noise on top. 
Essential foundation for any B2B SaaS outbound playbook before touching tools, cadences, or personalization tactics.
